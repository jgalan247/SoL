What Happens If Someone Steals Your Identity?

Identity theft can have devastating financial and emotional consequences.

  • If fraudsters gain direct access to your bank account or credit cards, they may leave you with no money or income
  • The more serious and complex the identity fraud, the longer it can take to recover any stolen funds
  • Identity fraud can affect your credit history. It is possible to correct this, but it can take a while to sort out and to demonstrate to your credit provider that you have been the victim of crime
  • It may make it harder for you to borrow money – a big problem if you are opening a new bank account, buying a car or applying for a mortgage
  • While it is possible for a victim to recoup stolen funds and clear their name, the emotional toll of identity theft can last far longer than any financial worries
  • Victims can blame themselves for their identity being stolen and feel more vulnerable to it happening again

Avoiding Identity Theft: Dev & Lena

Read the case study and discuss what could have been done differently.

Dev & Lena’s Story

Dev and his partner Lena have both been victims of identity theft in the last three years. They told their neighbours about this, and it turned out that several people who live in the same building have also been victims. All their letterboxes are on the street outside, so it is easy for thieves to access their mail, including bills and bank statements.

When a thief gets hold of a bill or bank statement they then have enough details to be able to apply for credit accounts (e.g. credit cards or shopping accounts) and then steal the cards or account details.

Dev only knew that this had happened to him when he received a statement showing that he owed a credit card company over £2,500. He didn’t have an account with this provider, but the thief had been able to take one out in his name and spend thousands of pounds that he was now being billed for. He had no idea what else they might have done with his personal details; they could have signed up to websites or even applied for a passport.

He contacted the credit card provider who helped him get the money back, but he found the whole experience very worrying.

💬 What could they have done?

What could Dev, Lena and their neighbours have done to avoid having their identities stolen?

  • Receive all statements and bills online
  • Check statements regularly and use secure apps to track spending
  • Check your credit report regularly

  • Pick up mail regularly or, if they go on holiday, ask a friend or a neighbour they know well to collect it

How to Protect Yourself from Identity Theft

Here are 8 essential tips to protect yourself. Click each one to mark it as understood.

  • ✓ 1. Never share passwords or PINs with others.
  • ✓ 2. Use strong passwords and PINs – don’t use family names; include a mix of upper and lower-case letters, numbers and symbols. Aim for a minimum of 8 characters.
  • ✓ 3. Do not use the same password or PIN for more than one account.
  • ✓ 4. Shred or burn any unwanted financial documents – do not put them straight into recycling.
  • ✓ 5. Make sure your computer has up to date firewall, anti-virus and anti-spyware programs.
  • ✓ 6. Limit the amount of personal information that you give away on social networking sites. Make use of any security settings offered.
  • ✓ 7. Only accept online friend requests from people who are familiar.
  • ✓ 8. Always review your bank account statements (and credit card statements when you get one).

Money Muling: Financial Crime

Money muling is a serious crime that particularly targets young people.

What is money muling? A money mule is someone who transfers illegally obtained money through their own bank account on behalf of someone else. Even if you don’t know the money is from crime, you are committing a criminal offence.

What are the consequences of money muling?

Consequences of money muling:

  • Up to 14 years in prison
  • A criminal record which can affect future employment, travel and education
  • Your bank account will be closed and you may find it very difficult to open a new one
  • Difficulty getting a mobile phone contract, mortgage or loan

Warning signs of money mule recruitment:

  • Being contacted out of the blue with an offer to make “easy money”
  • Being asked to receive money into your account and transfer it elsewhere
  • Job adverts for “money transfer agents” or “payment processors”
  • Being asked to use your own bank account for someone else’s money
  • Offers through social media, messaging apps or from people you’ve just met

Remember: If it sounds too good to be true, it probably is. Never allow anyone to use your bank account to transfer money. If you suspect you are being recruited as a money mule, report it to your bank and the police.
